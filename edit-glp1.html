GLP-1 & Medical Weight Management

What You Should Actually Know Before Starting GLP-1 Therapy

Semaglutide and tirzepatide have changed the conversation around weight management — but not every provider offering them has the same level of training, oversight, or follow-up care. Here's what to look for.

GLP-1 receptor agonists — the class of medications that includes semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound) — are among the most significant developments in medicine in a generation. For millions of women, they have offered a path to meaningful, sustained weight loss that diet and exercise alone could not deliver. But the speed at which they've entered mainstream culture has outpaced the availability of qualified, attentive providers.

Understanding what GLP-1 therapy actually involves — and what good care looks like — is the first step to finding a provider you can trust.

How GLP-1 Medications Work

GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) is a hormone naturally produced in the gut after eating. It signals the pancreas to release insulin, slows gastric emptying, and communicates satiety to the brain. GLP-1 receptor agonists mimic this hormone — reducing appetite, improving blood sugar regulation, and in clinical trials, producing average weight loss of 15–22% of body weight depending on the medication and dosage.

Unlike previous weight loss medications, GLP-1 drugs address the physiological drivers of hunger rather than simply suppressing it through stimulants. This is a meaningful distinction — and one reason outcomes have been more durable.

What Good GLP-1 Care Looks Like

The medication itself is only part of the picture. Well-managed GLP-1 therapy includes a thorough initial assessment — medical history, metabolic labs, cardiovascular risk factors, and a clear understanding of your goals. It also involves:

  • Gradual dose titration to minimize side effects
  • Regular follow-up appointments — not just prescription refills
  • Nutritional guidance to support lean muscle preservation during weight loss
  • Monitoring for contraindications and long-term safety considerations
  • A clear plan for what happens if you stop the medication

"The medication opens a window. What you do with that window — how you build sustainable habits, how you address the underlying metabolic picture — is where the provider's role becomes essential."

The Rise of Compounded GLP-1s

During periods of shortage, compounding pharmacies began producing versions of semaglutide and tirzepatide at lower price points. The FDA has since clarified its position on compounded GLP-1s as brand availability improves — but the market remains active and varied in quality.

If a provider is offering compounded GLP-1 therapy, the questions to ask are: which compounding pharmacy do they use, how is it dosed, and what monitoring is included? The answer tells you a great deal about the standard of care you can expect.

Red Flags to Watch For

The growth of telehealth GLP-1 prescribing has made medication more accessible — but it has also created a category of providers whose involvement begins and ends with writing a prescription. Be cautious of:

  • Providers who don't require labs before prescribing
  • No follow-up protocol or check-in schedule
  • No discussion of side effects, contraindications, or exit strategy
  • Unusually low pricing with no explanation of what's included

Finding the Right Provider

GLP-1 therapy is increasingly offered by primary care physicians, internists, endocrinologists, OB-GYNs, and nurse practitioners with appropriate training. What matters more than credential type is the quality of the relationship and the comprehensiveness of the care. A provider who takes time to understand your full health picture — not just your weight goal — is worth finding.
